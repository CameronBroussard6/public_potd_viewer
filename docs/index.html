Lajovic has certainly earned respect as a solid clay-courter throughout his 20-year career, but his best days are behind him at this point, and grass courts do not suit his game nearly as well. Dusan has never made it past the second round in 13 appearances here, and it's hard to imagine that he comes in with much confidence. Now, Lajovic has been given a chance to redeem himself, but it's going to take a really special performance for him to make a dent here. Taylor Fritz's reputation as a consistent top-10 performer was disrupted earlier this year, where he had some minor injury niggles that he was trying to play through, but a first-round loss to Nishesh Basavareddy at RG seems to have been a blessing in disguise. Fritz made finals in both Stuttgart and Halle, only losing out to some really special performances from Shelton and Tiafoe, and he made a wise decision to skip the final tune-up in Eastbourne. He's dropped some sets over the course of this grass-court season, but the reality is that all of those were to better grass-courters than Lajovic. Over the past few years, Fritz has developed his serve into one of the most consistent weapons on tour, and that pays dividends on grass courts. Lajovic's one-handed backhand comes with a pretty long backswing, which will make it even harder to make inroads into the Fritz serve. Both of these players will be looking to dominate with the serve and forehand, but the reality is that Fritz simply outclasses Lajovic in both of those departments. In theory, Lajovic's slice can be effective in long grass rallies, but he needs to stay competitive in the 0-4 shot range to even get there. To me, the most likely path for Fritz to lose a set is simply tiebreak variance as a result of being an exceptional server and subpar returner, and that's what we saw happen last year. However, Mpetshi Perricard and Diallo are much better servers than Lajovic, and I simply don't see that outcome as super likely. Taking the 3-0 in a first round can be dangerous, but I usually need to see something about the underdog's game that can pose a threat to convince me against it. In this case, I see Fritz moving on without too much trouble.
